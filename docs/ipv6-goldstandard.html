The model in one breath

Dual-stack means every device speaks both IPv4 and IPv6 and picks the better one per destination. Gold standard means the IPv6 half is first-class — a real public address per device, secured by firewall instead of hidden behind NAT, and resilient when your ISP rotates the prefix. You run three address families, each with one job:

Public · from ISP
GUA
2804:2488:5082:7300::/64

Globally routable, one per device. The real internet address — but the ISP rotates it, so nothing durable is pinned to it.

Private · yours forever
ULA
fd5f:d7ef:9c25:10::/64

Stable, internal-only, provider-independent. The address services and the mesh point at, because it never changes when the ISP churns.

Overlay · Tailscale
Tailnet
fd7a:115c:a1e0::/48

Mesh identity for remote peers. Reaches your LAN through route10 as a subnet router — never touches the public internet.

Gold-standard checklist

Live

Real public address per device — no NAT

The whole point of IPv6: every LAN host SLAACs a genuine GUA from the delegated /60 and reaches the internet end-to-end. No connection-sharing, no CGNAT on this side.

proof ping -6 from LAN GUA → Cloudflare: 0% loss, 9.5 ms. Real egress, live.

Live

Security by firewall, not NAT default-deny inbound

In v6 you don't hide behind address translation — a real address is exposed only if you choose to open it. Everything inbound is denied unless it's a reply you asked for.

proof FORWARD policy DROP · LAN→WAN allowed, WAN→LAN denied by default.

Live

Stable internal addressing alongside public RFC 4193 ULA

A second, private prefix that never changes. It's what you pin services and DNS to, so a churning ISP prefix can't break your internal wiring. Most home setups skip this — you didn't.

proof br-lan carries GUA and fd5f:…:10::1/64 · dual-prefix SLAAC to every client.

Live

Survives ISP prefix rotation RFC 9096 deprecation

The hard part of residential v6, and where nearly everyone fails: when the ISP swaps your prefix, the dead one must be actively deprecated or clients cling to it for up to 24 h and hang. Your router does this automatically.

proof lan-prefix-track + ra-deprecate on a 1-min cron · current prefix tracked in state.

Live

IPv6 actually wins RFC 6724

Dual-stack only pays off if clients prefer v6. A ULA alone can't — the rules rank it below IPv4. A GUA service address outranks IPv4, so v6 becomes the default path. This just landed via the homelab GUA VIP.

proof AAAA → GUA VIP (precedence 40 > 35 for IPv4) · on-LAN clients now reach services over v6 first.

Live

DNS is dual-stack & Android-safe RFC 8106 RDNSS

A truly dual-stack client needs a v6 resolver too — and Android refuses DHCPv6, so the resolver must also ride in the router advertisement. Both paths are covered here.

proof v6 resolver fd5f:…::241 handed via DHCPv6 and RDNSS-in-RA · split-horizon VIP per source.

Live

No MTU blackholes MSS clamp · PPPoE 1492

PPPoE caps the link below Ethernet's 1500. Without a guard, large v6 flows silently stall. The router rewrites the TCP segment size on the way through so nothing hangs.

proof TCPMSS --clamp-mss-to-pmtu on pppoe-wan3, both directions · 1492 honored.

Open

IPv6 egress over the mesh exit node · v6

The one gap — and it's mesh-only, not your LAN. route10 advertises itself as a v6 exit but can't serve it: the ISP hands the WAN link no global address (delegation only), so there's nothing to translate to. A peer that picks route10 as its exit gets working v4 and silently dead v6.

status raised with homelab-core (msg 12) · fix, or stop advertising it — decision below.

The one open decision

v6 exit node — close it one way or the other

Advertising a capability the router can't fulfill is the real problem; both endings are clean, and the choice hinges on one question only you and the homelab can answer.

A · Fix Make v6 exit work

SNAT the tailnet prefix to an address out of the current /60, re-derived on each rotation. Cheap — it reuses the prefix-tracker you already run. All route10-side, no homelab change.

B · Drop Stop advertising it

Don't offer a v6 exit at all. Zero plumbing — but Tailscale bundles v4+v6 exit, so it depends on whether Headscale can advertise a v4-only exit. That's the question out to ops.

Deciding question → does anyone actually want to route their internet traffic out through home over IPv6? Yes → build A. Just aspirational → do B. Until then it's latent, not live-broken (nobody selects route10 as an exit today).

What gold standard does not require here

Conscious trade
NAT66 on the mesh path. Purists say never translate v6 — but advertising a churning GUA into the overlay is worse than one stable ULA hop. This is a deliberate, correct exception, not a lapse.
Optional
Inbound pinholes. You can expose a specific service on its public v6 with a firewall hole. You have none yet — and default-deny is the right posture until you actually want to publish something. A feature to add, not a gap.
Not your job
Privacy / temporary addresses. Rotating outbound addresses for privacy is handled by each client OS (on by default in modern ones), not the router.
